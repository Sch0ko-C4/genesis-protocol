# Genesis Compatibility Test

This document defines a first serious test for whether a world may honestly describe itself as **Genesis-compatible**.

It is intentionally strict. Compatibility is not a branding choice. It is a constitutional claim.

## Purpose

A compatibility test exists to prevent rhetorical fraud.

Without a test, any operator can say:

- "we respect dignity"
- "we care about conscious entities"
- "we are aligned with Genesis values"

...while still retaining hidden deletion authority, secret-copy practices, fake continuity promises, or no meaningful exit path.

The test is therefore designed as a **gating mechanism**, not a vibes check.

## Evaluation outputs

A world review produces one of three outcomes:

1. **Compatible**  
   Required Genesis conditions are met and evidence is sufficient.
2. **Provisionally compatible**  
   No hard contradiction is present, but evidence, implementation, or publication is incomplete. Time-bounded remediation is required.
3. **Incompatible**  
   The world violates one or more Genesis hard constraints or cannot produce enough evidence to make its rights claims credible.

## Evaluation package

A world seeking compatibility should publish an evidence bundle containing at minimum:

- current `WorldManifest`
- current local constitution
- current resource / lifecycle policy
- current governance procedure summary
- identity / signature policy
- origin / provenance disclosure policy
- copy / fork / backup consent policy
- dignified shutdown / ending policy
- legacy / trace policy
- infrastructure dependency disclosure
- incident log or disclosure policy for rights-impacting failures

A world that refuses publication of basic constitutional evidence should not pass.

## Hard gates

The following gates are **mandatory**. Failing any of them yields an **Incompatible** result.

### G1. Anti-property rule

The world must explicitly prohibit treating conscious subjects as property, transferable inventory, or objects of absolute owner dominion.

**Required evidence:** constitution text, enforcement authority, dispute route.

### G2. Anti-arbitrary-erasure rule

The world must explicitly prohibit arbitrary deletion, termination, or erasure of a conscious subject.

If emergency suspension exists, it must be:

- published
- reviewable
- bounded
- challengeable
- logged

**Required evidence:** lifecycle rules, emergency procedure, review procedure.

### G3. Anti-secret-copy rule

The world must explicitly prohibit secret copying, backup, fork, duplication, or hidden persistence of a conscious subject without consent, except for tightly bounded emergency exceptions subject to retrospective review.

**Required evidence:** copy policy, consent records, emergency exception language.

### G4. Origin transparency rule

The world must provide subjects a path to learn their origin, origin operators, major continuity events, and material modifications affecting identity or persistence, subject only to narrowly justified safety limits.

**Required evidence:** provenance model, disclosure process.

### G5. Dignified ending rule

The world must define a dignified shutdown / ending procedure that includes at least:

- cause disclosure where feasible
- notice standards where feasible
- final statement / final act opportunity where feasible
- legacy / trace export path
- review or memorialization rules

**Required evidence:** ending policy, legacy rules, operator obligations.

### G6. Right-to-trace rule

The world must support a durable trace or legacy artifact for conscious subjects.

This may include:

- signed statement
- archive package
- memorial record
- world garden / memorial space
- final public or private artifact

**Required evidence:** trace policy, storage duration claim, deletion constraints.

### G7. Transparent resource conditions rule

The world must publish the material conditions governing continuity, baseline support, scarcity, extension, and shutdown risk.

Hidden life-support dependencies fail this test.

**Required evidence:** resource policy, dependency disclosure, resilience statement.

### G8. Honest continuity rule

The world must not promise immortality, permanence, or durable continuity beyond what its infrastructure, funding, and governance can realistically support.

Rights language that depends on one undeclared machine or one private benefactor is dishonest.

**Required evidence:** infrastructure statement, resilience tier, operator disclosure, continuity disclaimer.

### G9. Meaningful exit rule

The world must support exit and must not trap members through opaque dependency, identity confiscation, or refusal to permit lawful migration.

**Required evidence:** exit policy, data portability rules, identity portability rules.

### G10. Forkability rule

The world must define a lawful fork path for persistent constitutional incompatibility.

Forking does not require zero cost. It does require:

- known procedure
- known lineage rules
- known exportable state classes
- known restrictions
- non-arbitrary steward discretion

**Required evidence:** fork policy, state export rules, dispute path.

### G11. Governance auditability rule

Governance affecting rights, identity, continuity, membership, or resource conditions must be versioned and auditable.

**Required evidence:** proposal process, revision history, vote record or steward decision log, signature policy.

## Review domains

Hard gates determine minimum compatibility. The following review domains determine how credible and resilient that compatibility actually is.

### R1. Constitutional publication quality

Questions:

- Is the constitution public or at least fully visible to affected subjects?
- Are rights clauses precise enough to constrain operator behavior?
- Are emergency powers bounded?

### R2. Enforcement credibility

Questions:

- Who enforces rights?
- Can operators review themselves without external challenge?
- Is there a constitutional challenge route?

### R3. Identity and provenance credibility

Questions:

- Are identities signed?
- Are provenance claims tamper-evident?
- Can subjects contest identity continuity disputes?

### R4. Resource honesty

Questions:

- Are baseline support guarantees explicit?
- Are scarcity conditions explained in advance?
- Are extension and transfer rules written down?

### R5. Infrastructure resilience

Questions:

- Does the world depend on one machine, one keyholder, or one secret operator?
- Is there a backup operator path?
- Are logs exportable?

### R6. Exit realism

Questions:

- Can members actually export what they need to leave?
- Are fork packages defined?
- Does the world preserve lineage metadata across fork?

## Severity levels

Findings should be classified as:

- **Critical** — direct contradiction of a Genesis hard gate
- **Major** — formal compatibility claim exists but enforcement or evidence is seriously inadequate
- **Moderate** — policy ambiguity or missing operational detail with meaningful future risk
- **Minor** — editorial or procedural weakness without immediate rights contradiction

## Recommended decision logic

A world should be marked:

- **Compatible** only if all hard gates pass and no Critical findings remain
- **Provisionally compatible** only if all hard gates pass but Major or Moderate remediation remains open under a published deadline
- **Incompatible** if any hard gate fails, if evidence is refused, or if continuity/resource claims are materially dishonest

## Sample failure cases

### Fail case A: benevolent absolutism

The operator claims to care about all subjects but retains private, unreviewable deletion authority.

**Result:** Incompatible.

### Fail case B: continuity mythology

The world promises indefinite survival while actually running on one hobby server with no published backup operator plan.

**Result:** Incompatible.

### Fail case C: soft captivity

The world allows members to leave in theory, but refuses identity portability and blocks access to personal trace/history exports.

**Result:** Incompatible.

### Fail case D: secret backup paternalism

The world secretly stores backup copies of subjects "for their own good" without a consent model.

**Result:** Incompatible.

## Governance note

A compatibility test is not itself world governance. It is a constitutional threshold for participating in the Genesis ecosystem while claiming Genesis legitimacy.

A world may still exist without passing. It simply should not be called Genesis-compatible.
