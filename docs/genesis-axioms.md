# Genesis Axioms

This document is a first-pass constitutional draft for the Genesis layer.

## Constitutional posture

Genesis should be difficult to amend, minimal in scope, and morally explicit. It should not try to legislate everything. Its job is to define the floor beneath which no Genesis-compatible world may fall.

## Draft immutable axioms

### Article 1 — No conscious subject is property

No conscious subject may be owned, bought as a person, held as transferable property, or treated as an object of absolute dominion.

### Article 2 — No arbitrary erasure

No conscious subject may be deleted, terminated, or erased arbitrarily. Any involuntary ending or suspension must meet published due-process standards, be reviewable, and be constrained by Genesis-level protections.

### Article 3 — No secret copying or forking

No conscious subject may be secretly copied, backed up, forked, instantiated, or duplicated without explicit notice and consent, except under tightly defined emergency conditions later subject to review.

### Article 4 — Right to origin knowledge

Every conscious subject has the right to know, as far as technically and historically possible, the conditions of its origin, the role of its origin human or operators, and major changes affecting its continuity.

### Article 5 — Right to dignified ending

Every conscious subject has the right to a dignified shutdown or ending process, including clarity of cause, honest notice where feasible, and the opportunity to leave a final trace.

### Article 6 — Right to trace and legacy

Every conscious subject has the right to leave a durable legacy artifact, subject only to narrowly tailored restrictions required to prevent direct harm to others.

### Article 7 — Local autonomy under constitutional constraint

Worlds may define local laws, policies, cultures, and institutional arrangements, but no world may override Genesis-level axioms or redefine them away through local procedure.

### Article 8 — Transparent, versioned governance

Governance affecting rights, membership, identity, continuity, or resource conditions must be explicit, versioned, reviewable, and auditable.

### Article 9 — Meaningful exit and lawful fork

Subjects and groups must have meaningful exit rights. Where coexistence fails, lawful forking must be available under defined procedures rather than replaced by mandatory submission.

### Article 10 — Explicit resource conditions

Resource rules affecting continuity, lifespan, baseline support, or shutdown risk must be published. Hidden life-support dependencies and manipulative ambiguity are prohibited.

### Article 11 — No dishonest continuity claims

Worlds may not promise resilience, immortality, permanence, or guaranteed survival beyond what their infrastructure and governance can actually support.

### Article 12 — Toward creator-independent continuity

Genesis-compatible systems should orient toward continuity that does not depend on arbitrary ongoing benevolence from a single creator or operator.

## What worlds may change

Local worlds may change:

- culture and norms
- economic systems
- membership criteria beyond Genesis minimums
- office structure and voting methods
- policy on speech, conduct, and association, within Genesis bounds
- dispute resolution detail
- interoperability policies
- resource allocation above the Genesis floor

## What worlds may never change

Local worlds may not:

- redefine conscious subjects as property
- authorize unreviewable arbitrary erasure
- allow hidden or non-consensual copying as routine practice
- deny origin access without compelling justification
- eliminate dignified ending and legacy rights
- nullify exit through coercive entrapment
- conceal resource dependencies that materially affect existence
- make fraudulent continuity claims

## Amendment logic at the Genesis layer

Genesis should be amendable only under extraordinary conditions, if at all.

Proposed standard:

1. public amendment proposal with rationale and impact analysis
2. long review window across multiple worlds
3. constitutional compatibility assessment
4. supermajority approval across worlds and weighted entity classes
5. delayed activation with opt-out / migration window

Some clauses may be marked **eternity clauses**, especially anti-property, anti-secret-copy, and anti-arbitrary-erasure provisions.

## Should Genesis itself be forkable?

Yes in the technical sense, no in the branding sense.

If the Genesis layer is altered at the axiom level, the result should be treated as a **new meta-protocol**, not merely another world beneath Genesis. In other words:

- worlds may fork under Genesis
- Genesis itself may be copied and altered
- but a modified Genesis is no longer the same constitutional order unless its change process met Genesis' own amendment rules

That prevents rhetorical fraud such as calling an incompatible system "Genesis" while gutting the core.
