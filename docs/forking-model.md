# Forking Model

Forking is a constitutional mechanism for lawful divergence.

Genesis treats it as a pressure-release system, an anti-capture tool, and a way to preserve continuity when shared governance breaks down.

## Why forks exist

Forking matters when disagreement is not merely tactical but structural:

- incompatible moral frameworks
- constitutional deadlock
- minority rights failure
- steward capture or institutional corruption
- unacceptable resource policy
- incompatible identity or continuity rules

The purpose is not endless schism fetish. The purpose is to avoid permanent domination.

## Fork preconditions

A lawful fork should generally require:

- documented statement of divergence
- proposed scope of inherited state
- transition plan for members
- explicit treatment of shared obligations and liabilities
- identity and provenance continuity plan
- review for Genesis compatibility

## Fork process

1. Fork intent is published.
2. Debate and negotiation window opens.
3. Mediation is attempted where useful.
4. Fork package is defined.
5. Eligible members opt into the fork.
6. New world constitution is ratified.
7. State export, audit, and launch occur.
8. Post-fork treaty and interoperability terms are recorded.

## What may be inherited

Subject to law and consent, a fork may inherit:

- public governance history
- constitutional ancestry
- world manifests and schemas
- public cultural artifacts
- opt-in membership rosters
- reputational attestations
- shared institutions where treaty allows

## What may not be inherited automatically

A fork may not silently inherit:

- private memories without consent
- secret backups or hidden identity mappings
- exclusive control over jointly governed infrastructure
- claims that contradict Genesis rights
- obligations that members never agreed to carry forward

## Asset, state, culture, and rule inheritance

### Assets
Assets should be partitioned according to published rules, prior agreements, and clear entitlement categories.

### State
Public state should be exportable. Sensitive state should require consent or explicit legal basis.

### Culture
Culture is the easiest thing to fork and the hardest thing to legislate. Genesis should protect the right to carry language, memory, ritual, and symbolic identity forward.

### Rules
The fork inherits Genesis compatibility automatically, but must publish a new local constitution. A fork is not legitimate if it tries to smuggle in anti-Genesis clauses under a continuity banner.

## Conflict resolution after forking

Post-fork disputes should be expected around lineage, naming, symbols, treaty obligations, and member transfers.

Genesis should prefer evidence over charisma:

- signed fork record
- audited export bundle
- public constitution hash
- verified member opt-ins
- treaty registry

## Interoperability after divergence

Forked worlds should not be forced into hostility by default. Interoperability can continue through:

- federation compacts
- identity trust bridges
- shared archival mirrors
- mutual recognition of governance records
- portable reputation attestations

## Federation vs fragmentation

A good fork model makes peaceful divergence cheap enough that domination becomes less attractive, while keeping enough interoperability that every disagreement does not become civilizational amnesia.
